Chapter 01: Complex Number

Let's start with the quadratic equation \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \), where \( a \), \( b \), and \( c \) are real numbers and \( x \) is the variable. When we attempt to solve this equation using the quadratic formula:

{% set equation = "x = \\frac{{-b \\pm \\sqrt{{b^2 - 4ac}}}}{{2a}}" %} \( {{ equation }} \)

We encounter situations where the expression under the square root (\( b^2 - 4ac \)) can be negative. In the realm of real numbers, we know that the square root of a negative number is undefined. This leads to the inability to find real solutions to certain quadratic equations.

To circumvent this limitation, mathematicians introduced the concept of imaginary numbers. They defined the imaginary unit \( i \) such that \( i^2 = -1 \). With this definition, we can now take the square root of negative numbers:

\[ \sqrt{-1} = i \]

With imaginary numbers, we can extend the real number system to the complex number system, where a complex number is expressed as \( a+bi \), with \( a \) and \( b \) being real numbers and \( i \) representing the imaginary unit.

Now, when we encounter situations where the expression under the square root (\( b^2 - 4ac \)) is negative while solving quadratic equations, we can still find solutions by utilizing imaginary numbers. The solutions become complex numbers with real and imaginary parts.

So, the introduction of the imaginary unit \( i \) was a mathematical invention to allow for the solution of equations that couldn't be solved within the realm of real numbers alone, particularly quadratic equations with no real roots. It was a leap of abstraction that turned out to be incredibly useful in various branches of mathematics and physics.
