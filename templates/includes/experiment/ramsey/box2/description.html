The Ramsey Interferometer

Norman Ramsey's method of separated oscillatory fields (1949, Nobel Prize 1989) is the technique at the heart of every atomic clock. Instead of driving a two-level atom with one long pulse, it uses two short \( \pi/2 \) pulses separated by a period of free evolution of duration \( T \). It is a quantum interferometer in which the two “paths” are the ground and excited states of the atom.

How it works

  1. The atom begins in the ground state \( |g\rangle \).
  2. The first \( \pi/2 \) pulse places it in an equal superposition \( \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|g\rangle + |e\rangle) \) — on the Bloch sphere the state now lies on the equator.
  3. During the free time \( T \), the superposition precesses. If the drive is detuned from the atomic frequency by \( \delta \), the two components accumulate a relative phase \( \phi = \delta T \).
  4. The second \( \pi/2 \) pulse converts that accumulated phase into a population difference that a detector can read out.

The probability of finding the atom in the excited state is:

\( P_e(\delta) = \cos^2\!\left(\dfrac{\delta T}{2}\right) = \tfrac{1}{2}\big(1 + \cos(\delta T)\big) \)

Plotting \( P_e \) against the detuning \( \delta \) gives the Ramsey fringes shown in the simulator. Their spacing is \( \Delta\delta = 2\pi / T \): the longer the free-evolution time T, the narrower the central fringe, and the more precisely the drive frequency can be locked to the atomic transition. This is why a longer interrogation time means a more accurate clock.

Compared with driving a single long pulse (a Rabi measurement), Ramsey's separated-pulse scheme is far less sensitive to fluctuations in the pulse amplitude and to the field's uniformity, because the atom spends most of its time evolving freely rather than being driven — which is what makes it so precise.
