The Stern–Gerlach Experiment

In 1922, Otto Stern and Walther Gerlach sent a beam of silver atoms through a strong, inhomogeneous magnetic field. Each atom carries a magnetic moment, so classically the field should deflect atoms by an amount proportional to the component of that moment along the field — and since the moments point in every direction, the beam should spread into a single continuous smear on the detector.

Instead, the beam split into two discrete spots. The component of angular momentum along the measurement axis is quantised: for a spin-½ particle it can only take the values \( +\tfrac{\hbar}{2} \) (“up”) or \( -\tfrac{\hbar}{2} \) (“down”).

Sequential measurements — what the simulator shows

Prepare an atom to be spin-up along Z using a first apparatus. Now measure it again with a second Stern–Gerlach magnet whose axis \( \hat{n} \) is rotated by an angle \( \theta \) from Z. The state \( |{\uparrow_z}\rangle \) written in the basis of the tilted apparatus is:

\( |{\uparrow_z}\rangle = \cos\tfrac{\theta}{2}\,|{\uparrow_n}\rangle + \sin\tfrac{\theta}{2}\,|{\downarrow_n}\rangle \)

so the probabilities of the two outcomes are given by the Born rule:

\( P(\uparrow_n) = \cos^2\!\tfrac{\theta}{2}, \qquad P(\downarrow_n) = \sin^2\!\tfrac{\theta}{2} \)

  • θ = 0°: the two magnets agree — every atom comes out “up” (P = 1). The result is repeatable.
  • θ = 90°: a Z-up atom has no definite value along the new axis; the outcome is a perfect 50/50 coin flip. Measuring along X has also erased the original Z information.
  • θ = 180°: the second magnet is upside-down, so “up” and “down” swap — every atom now comes out “down” (P = 1).

The half-angle \( \theta/2 \) is the fingerprint of spin-½: you must rotate the apparatus by a full \( 360^\circ \) — not \( 180^\circ \) — to return to the original probabilities. This experiment is the cleanest demonstration that measurement in quantum mechanics is probabilistic, that observables can be incompatible, and that a measurement changes the state it acts on.
